# Team calibration (reference)

## Team Calibration (when reviewing multiple engineers)

If reviewing more than one engineer, after completing individual assessments, produce a team
calibration view and apply the checks below.

**Calibration table:**

```
Engineer | Seniority | Craftsmanship | Architecture | Autonomy | Leadership | Impact | Overall | Trajectory
```

**Check 1 — Compression / clustering:**

Are multiple engineers rated identically across all or most competencies? Compression is the
most common calibration failure — it occurs when a manager avoids differentiation to prevent
difficult conversations. Ask:
- Is the clustering real (engineers are genuinely performing similarly) or have ratings been
  softened to avoid conflict?
- If two engineers are at the same level in every competency, what specifically distinguishes
  them? If the answer is "nothing," that is worth examining — it is rare for two engineers to
  have identical profiles across five dimensions.
- Look for compression at the top (everyone rated ADVANCED) and the bottom (everyone rated
  PRACTITIONER). Both are warning signs.

**Check 2 — Halo patterns:**

Is one engineer rated uniformly high or uniformly low across all five competencies? Uniform
ratings almost always reflect a general impression of the engineer rather than genuine
per-competency calibration. For each engineer with a uniform profile, revisit each competency
independently and ask: "What specific evidence do I have for this competency specifically?"
If the answer is the same example reused across multiple competencies, flag it.

**Check 3 — Role-adjusted comparison:**

Do not compare product/feature engineers and platform/internal engineers on the same evidence
baseline. Their competency profiles will naturally look different — e.g. a platform engineer
with strong Architecture but modest user-facing Impact is not necessarily weaker than a
product engineer with the inverse profile. When presenting team calibration, note where role
differences explain profile differences.

**Check 4 — Promotion contention:**

If two or more engineers appear to be at or near promotion readiness, address this explicitly:
- Are both genuinely performing above their current level, or is one a stronger case?
- Is there capacity to promote both, or is there a sequencing question?
- Name the specific competency evidence that distinguishes them — do not leave the comparison
  implicit. The manager will need to articulate this in a calibration conversation.

**Check 5 — At-risk signals:**

Flag any engineer showing:
- Plateauing trajectory: strong historical performance but no new evidence of growth or
  increased scope in the last review period
- Declining trajectory: evidence of regression — reduced output quality, increased reactive
  ops burden they caused, peer feedback deteriorating
- Level mismatch risk: performing at a level below their seniority band for more than one
  review cycle — which may require a more direct performance conversation

**Framing relative comparisons in writing:**

When the calibration output references multiple engineers, do not rank them explicitly or
use comparative language that could feel like a zero-sum assessment. Each engineer's
assessment should stand on its own evidence. The calibration view is for the manager's
internal use — it should not appear verbatim in engineer-facing review documents.
